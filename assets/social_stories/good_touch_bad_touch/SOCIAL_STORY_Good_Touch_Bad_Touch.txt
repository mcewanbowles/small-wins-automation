## PAGE 5: BAD TOUCHES

**TEXT:**
```
WHAT ARE BAD TOUCHES?

Bad touches:
• Feel uncomfortable or wrong
• Hurt me
• Make me scared or confused
• Touch my private parts (except medical care or hygiene help)

EXAMPLES OF BAD TOUCHES:

• Anyone touching my private parts (not doctor or parent for care)
• Anyone asking me to touch their private parts
• Hitting, pushing, or hurting me
• Touching that makes me uncomfortable
• Unwanted hugs or kisses
• Anyone touching me when I say NO

BAD TOUCHES CAN COME FROM:
• Strangers
• People I know
• Friends
• Family members
• Teachers or coaches
• ANYONE

Even if I know the person, bad touch is still bad!

IMPORTANT:
If someone does a bad touch:
• It is NOT my fault
• I did nothing wrong
• I need to tell a trusted adult
• I should keep telling until someone helps
```

## PAGE 7: SAYING NO TO TOUCHES

**TEXT:**
```
I CAN SAY NO!

I have the right to say NO to touches, even if:
• The person is nice
• The person is family
• The person is a friend
• I usually like hugs from them

HOW TO SAY NO:

Words I can use:
• "No thank you"
• "I don't want a hug right now"
• "Please don't touch me"
• "Stop"
• "I don't like that"

Body language:
• Step away
• Put hands up (stop signal)
• Shake my head
• Move to a different spot

IT'S OKAY TO SAY NO!

Saying no to touches doesn't mean:
• I'm being rude
• I'm being mean
• I don't like the person

It means:
• I'm taking care of my body
• I'm setting boundaries
• I'm making my own choices

GOOD PEOPLE WILL RESPECT MY NO!

If someone gets mad when I say no to a touch:
• They are not respecting my boundaries
• I should tell a trusted adult
• Good people understand "no"
```

## PAGE 8: SECRETS vs SURPRISES

**TEXT:**
```
SECRETS vs SURPRISES

There's a difference between secrets and surprises!

SURPRISES:
• Make people happy
• Get told eventually (like surprise party)
• Feel exciting and fun
• Adults know about them
• It's okay to keep surprises!

Examples:
• Birthday surprise party
• Secret gift
• Surprise trip
• Special surprise for someone

SECRETS (Bad kind):
• Make me feel uncomfortable
• I'm told to NEVER tell
• Feel scary or confusing
• Adults don't know
• Often involve touches or breaking rules

Examples of BAD secrets:
• "Don't tell anyone I touched you"
• "This is our secret" (about touching)
• "Your parents will be mad if you tell"
• "I'll hurt you if you tell"
• "No one will believe you"

IMPORTANT RULE:

If someone tells me to keep a secret about touching, I should:
• Tell a trusted adult RIGHT AWAY
• It's okay to break that kind of secret
• The person who told me to keep the secret is wrong
• I will not get in trouble for telling

GOOD TOUCHES DON'T NEED SECRETS!

If someone says "This is our secret" about touching:
• That is a warning sign
• I need to tell an adult immediately
• That person is doing something wrong
```

GUIDE FOR PARENTS & EDUCATORS

WHY THIS TOPIC IS CRITICAL:

Children with autism/special needs are at HIGHER RISK for abuse:
• 3-4 times more likely to be abused than typically developing peers
• May not recognize warning signs
• May have difficulty communicating about abuse
• May not understand social boundaries
• Often taught to comply with adults without question

This social story provides critical protection!

---

## TEACHING TIPS:

1. **START EARLY, REVIEW OFTEN**
   - Begin teaching around age 4-5
   - Review multiple times per year
   - Make it ongoing conversation, not one-time talk
   - Adapt language for developmental level

2. **USE CORRECT ANATOMICAL TERMS**
   - Say "penis," "vagina," "breasts," "bottom"
   - Don't use cute nicknames for private parts
   - This helps child report abuse clearly
   - Medical terms are less ambiguous

3. **TEACH BODY AUTONOMY**
   - Don't force child to hug/kiss relatives
   - Respect their "no" to physical affection
   - Model asking permission before touching
   - Let them have control over their body

4. **PRACTICE SCENARIOS**
   - Role-play saying "NO"
   - Practice telling a trusted adult
   - Act out different scenarios
   - Make it concrete and specific

5. **CREATE SAFETY NETWORK**
   - Identify multiple trusted adults
   - Make sure child knows who they can tell
   - Include people outside family (in case abuser is family)
   - Have backup adults

---

## RED FLAGS TO WATCH FOR:

Warning signs of possible abuse:
⚠️ Regression in behavior (toileting accidents, baby talk)
⚠️ Fear of specific people or places
⚠️ Sexualized behavior inappropriate for age
⚠️ Knowledge of sexual acts beyond development
⚠️ Secrecy about activities with specific person
⚠️ Unexplained injuries, especially in private areas
⚠️ Changes in mood (withdrawn, anxious, depressed)
⚠️ Sleep problems or nightmares
⚠️ Doesn't want to be alone with specific person
⚠️ Hints or tries to tell about "secrets"

If you notice ANY of these: Take it seriously and investigate!

---

## IF CHILD DISCLOSES ABUSE:

DO:
✓ Stay calm (don't alarm the child)
✓ Believe them
✓ Say "Thank you for telling me"
✓ Reassure "It's not your fault"
✓ Reassure "I will keep you safe"
✓ Report to authorities immediately
✓ Document what child said (exact words if possible)
✓ Limit questions (let professionals interview)

DON'T:
✗ Panic or overreact
✗ Ask leading questions
✗ Promise to keep it secret
✗ Confront the abuser yourself
✗ Doubt or question child's account
✗ Blame the child in any way

---

## REPORTING REQUIREMENTS:

**MANDATORY REPORTING:**
In most areas, teachers, coaches, doctors, therapists are REQUIRED to report suspected abuse.

**HOW TO REPORT:**
• Child Protective Services (CPS)
• Police department
• National Child Abuse Hotline: 1-800-422-4453

Don't try to investigate yourself - report and let professionals handle it!

## ADDRESSING COMMON CONCERNS:

**"Won't this scare my child?"**
- Presented matter-of-factly, it empowers rather than scares
- Fear comes from NOT knowing what to do
- Knowledge = protection

**"What about affection from family?"**
- Child can still hug grandma IF THEY WANT TO
- Key is: THEIR choice
- Don't force physical affection even with safe family
- "Grandma would love a hug if you'd like to give one!"

**"What if they say NO to everyone?"**
- This is healthy boundary-setting!
- Respect it
- Offer alternatives ("High five instead?")
- They're practicing autonomy

---

## PREVENTION STRATEGIES:

**REDUCE RISK:**
1. Know who has access to your child
2. Supervise interactions with adults
3. Teach these safety rules early and often
4. Listen when child seems uncomfortable with someone
5. Don't force affection
6. Respect their privacy (knock before entering)
7. Monitor online activity
8. Build open communication

**SAFE ADULTS:**
Abusers often:
- Seek access to vulnerable children
- Groom child and family
- Use positions of trust (coach, teacher, family friend)

EVEN TRUSTED ADULTS CAN ABUSE!
Teach child they can tell about ANYONE, even people you trust.
