# Evidence-Based 5-Level Sequencing System for Special Education

## Overview

This document outlines the research-based 5-level progression for the Brown Bear sequencing activity, designed specifically for special education (SPED) students. The system integrates authentic photographs, follows scaffolding theory, and includes a complete independence/assessment level.

## Theoretical Framework

### Scaffolding and Gradual Release of Responsibility

**Scaffolding** provides temporary, targeted supports that help students accomplish tasks they cannot yet achieve independently. The **Gradual Release of Responsibility** model systematically reduces support as students build competence.

**Key Principles:**
1. Start with maximum support (errorless learning)
2. Gradually fade supports based on student mastery
3. Use multiple modality scaffolds (visual, verbal, written)
4. Monitor progress through formative assessment
5. Build toward complete independence
6. Align with IEP goals and individual needs

## Evidence-Based 5-Level Progression

### Level 1: Color PCS Symbol Watermarks (Maximum Support)
**Color:** Orange (#F4B400)  
**Support Type:** Errorless learning with symbolic representation

**Features:**
- Watermark image hints (15% opacity) showing correct answer in each box
- Full color, detailed PCS® symbols
- Visual cues prevent errors and build confidence
- Students can succeed independently while learning sequence

**Pedagogical Rationale:**
- **Errorless learning** reduces frustration and anxiety (Coul, S., et al., 2020)
- Visual scaffolds are concrete and accessible for non-readers
- Familiar symbols provide consistency with AAC systems
- Builds confidence and positive learning associations
- Appropriate for initial learning and students with higher support needs

**Evidence Base:**
- Council for Exceptional Children High-Leverage Practice #15: Provide Scaffolded Supports
- Errorless learning methodology for skill acquisition (Smith, 2001)
- Visual cue research (AFIRM Autism Focused Intervention Resources & Modules, 2024)

---

### Level 2: Real Photograph Watermarks (Generalization Support)
**Color:** Blue (#4285F4)  
**Support Type:** Authentic imagery for generalization ← **NEW**

**Features:**
- Real photograph watermarks (15% opacity) of actual animals/people
- Introduces authentic, realistic representations
- Still provides visual scaffolding like Level 1
- Bridges symbolic and real-world understanding

**Pedagogical Rationale:**
- **Generalization training** is critical for SPED students (Stokes & Baer, 1977)
- Real photos help students connect symbols to actual objects/beings
- Supports transition from AAC symbols to environmental stimuli
- Maintains support level while changing stimulus type
- Prepares for real-world application of sequencing skills
- Essential for functional life skills development

**Evidence Base:**
- Generalization research shows need for varied exemplars (Gast et al., 2014)
- Authentic materials improve real-world transfer (Browder et al., 2012)
- Visual supports with photographs enhance comprehension (Quill, 1995)
- Real images support students with autism spectrum disorders (Hodgdon, 1995)

**Why Real Photos at Level 2:**
- Provides same level of support as Level 1 (watermarks)
- Changes stimulus type without changing support amount
- Teaches students that "brown bear" can be symbol OR photograph
- Critical for generalization before reducing support further

---

### Level 3: Black & White PCS Symbols (Reduced Visual Support)
**Color:** Green (#34A853)  
**Support Type:** Moderate scaffolding with increased discrimination

**Features:**
- Black and white line-drawn PCS® symbols (no color cues)
- Removes color AND photo realism as matching strategies
- Forces attention to form, shape, and character details
- Still provides visual reference but requires more discrimination

**Pedagogical Rationale:**
- **Systematic fading** removes easiest cues first (Cooper et al., 2020)
- Removes both color (easy cue) and realism (concrete support)
- Helps students focus on distinctive features
- B&W reduces visual complexity for some learners
- Generalizes better to print materials and worksheets
- Intermediate step toward text-based independence

**Evidence Base:**
- Visual scaffolding research shows B&W reduces distractions (Carnine, 1980)
- Fading strategy: remove perceptual cues systematically
- Used widely in assessment and generalization phases
- Prepares for academic worksheets which are often B&W

**Why B&W at Level 3:**
- Requires students to use shape discrimination, not just color matching
- Tests whether student has internalized character features
- Removes two scaffolds simultaneously (color + realism)
- Bigger challenge than Level 2, preparing for text-only

---

### Level 4: Text Labels Only (Minimal Visual Support)
**Color:** Purple (#9334E6)  
**Support Type:** Literacy-based, language-focused

**Features:**
- **Text labels only** (e.g., "Brown Bear", "Red Bird")
- No visual image hints whatsoever
- Requires reading/word recognition OR receptive language
- Most abstract level of visual support
- Students must rely on story knowledge and literacy skills

**Pedagogical Rationale:**
- Aligns with **gradual release of responsibility** model
- Promotes literacy skills and word recognition
- Most similar to traditional academic expectations
- Prepares for text-based academic content
- Can be used with read-aloud support for non-readers
- Tests story knowledge without visual props

**Evidence Base:**
- Literacy instruction for students with disabilities (Copeland & Keefe, 2007)
- Text-based supports promote academic inclusion
- Aligns with Universal Design for Learning principles
- Supports common core literacy standards

**Why Text at Level 4:**
- Removes all visual scaffolds except written words
- Requires either literacy OR very strong story memory
- Significant jump from B&W images
- Still provides linguistic support (not completely blank)

---

### Level 5: No Help - Blank Boxes (Complete Independence)
**Color:** Red (#EA4335)  
**Support Type:** None - assessment of mastery ← **NEW**

**Features:**
- **Completely blank boxes** - no hints, images, or text
- Student must sequence purely from memory and story knowledge
- Represents true independence and mastery
- Assessment-ready level for progress monitoring
- Demonstrates generalization to novel contexts

**Pedagogical Rationale:**
- **Ultimate independence** is the goal of all scaffolding (Wood et al., 1976)
- Assesses whether student has truly internalized the sequence
- Shows mastery without any external supports
- Can function as formative or summative assessment
- Demonstrates skill maintenance over time
- Proves generalization to completely novel contexts

**Evidence Base:**
- Assessment of skill mastery requires performance without supports (Wolery et al., 1992)
- Independence is criterion for IEP goal achievement
- Maintenance checks require no-support conditions
- Generalization assessed with minimal-to-no prompting (Haring & Liberty, 1990)

**Why Blank Boxes at Level 5:**
- True test of whether student has learned the sequence
- Can be used for:
  - Baseline assessment (before instruction)
  - Progress monitoring (during instruction)
  - Mastery assessment (after instruction)  
  - Maintenance checks (weeks/months later)
- Prepares for real-world situations with no external supports
- Demonstrates complete independence

## Progression Summary

| Level | Support Type | Scaffolds Provided | Skills Required |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Color Symbol Watermarks | Maximum | Visual matching to familiar symbols |
| 2 | Real Photo Watermarks | Maximum (different modality) | Visual matching to real images, generalization |
| 3 | B&W Symbols | Moderate | Shape discrimination, form recognition |
| 4 | Text Labels | Minimal | Literacy OR strong story memory |
| 5 | Blank Boxes | None | Complete story knowledge, sequencing from memory |

## Implementation Guidelines

### Student Placement

**Level 1 (Color Symbols):**
- Initial learning phase
- Students with higher support needs
- Building confidence and preventing frustration
- Learning the basic story sequence

**Level 2 (Real Photos):**
- Ready to generalize beyond symbols
- Succeeded with Level 1
- Need bridge to real-world representations
- Working on functional life skills

**Level 3 (B&W Symbols):**
- Mastered matching with color cues
- Ready for increased challenge
- Can discriminate shapes and forms
- Preparing for academic materials

**Level 4 (Text Labels):**
- Emerging or established literacy skills
- Strong story knowledge
- Ready for text-based learning
- Working toward academic inclusion

**Level 5 (Blank Boxes):**
- Demonstrated mastery at Levels 1-4
- Ready for independence check
- Assessment purposes
- Proving skill maintenance

### Progression Strategy

**Advancement Criteria:**
- 80-90% accuracy across 2-3 sessions at current level
- Consistent performance without additional prompting
- Maintained attention throughout activity
- Decreased time to complete (shows automaticity)

**Regression Strategy:**
- If student struggles at new level, return to previous level
- Provide 1-2 more sessions at mastered level
- Try new level again with additional verbal support
- Consider individual student factors (fatigue, motivation, etc.)

### Progress Monitoring

**Data Collection:**
- Record accuracy at each level
- Note type of errors (order, character substitution, etc.)
- Document time to completion
- Track need for additional prompts/supports

**IEP Goal Example:**
"[Student] will independently sequence the 11 events in the Brown Bear story with 90% accuracy across 3 consecutive sessions as measured by teacher observation, moving from Level 1 (color symbol watermarks) to Level 5 (no supports) by [date]."

This 5-level progression provides a complete, evidence-based pathway from maximum support to full independence. The inclusion of **real photographs** (Level 2) addresses the critical need for generalization training, while the **blank boxes** (Level 5) provide an authentic assessment of mastery.

- ✅ Grounded in scaffolding and errorless learning research
- ✅ Aligned with High-Leverage Practices for special education
- ✅ Appropriate for diverse learner needs and abilities
- ✅ Provides clear progression for IEP goal development
- ✅ Includes both instructional and assessment components
- ✅ Supports generalization from symbols to real-world contexts
- ✅ Builds toward complete independence and mastery
